Portfolio #2: Data, Information & IS

Concepts and Support Systems

View Original PDF

Data (The Raw Facts)

  • Raw, unorganized facts (symbols, numbers).
  • Factual but has no meaning until processed.
  • Can be Qualitative (descriptive) or Quantitative (numerical).

Information (The Meaning)

  • Data that has been transformed and given meaning.
  • The output of data processing.
  • Allows sharing knowledge and is meaningful to the receiver.
Data Information
Always factual.Can be wrong (if processed incorrectly).
Raw information.Processed Data that is meaningful.
Qualitative or Quantitative.Presented in words, numbers, images, or documents.
Independent.Dependent on data.
No inherent meaning by itself.Has meaning because it is structured and interpreted.

Support Systems in IS

TPS

Transaction Processing

Processes daily transactions like sales and payroll. Used in banking and retail.

MIS

Management Information

Bridges data and tactical decisions. Provides performance monitoring reports.

DSS

Decision Support

Solves non-routine problems using analytics. Essential for risk management.

ESS

Executive Support

Tailored for senior management strategy. Used for competitor analysis.

OAS

Office Automation

Optimizes routine tasks like email and documentation to streamline workflow.

BIS

Business Intelligence

Transforms data into competitive insights and predictive analytics.

My Reflection

This portfolio clarified the process of transforming raw data into actionable decisions. A key takeaway was learning to distinguish neutral data from meaningful information‚Äîan essential skill for avoiding errors. I learned that information systems are integrated frameworks of hardware, people, and processes embedded within organizations. Studying support systems such as TPS and DSS demonstrated how technology scales to meet different levels of organizational responsibility.

Portfolio #5: Motherboards

Form Factors and Design

View Original PDF

The motherboard is the foundational backbone connecting all hardware. The Form Factor dictates size, layout, and compatibility.

Comparison of Motherboard Form Factors

MotherboardBuild TypeCPU/RAMExpansion
Standard ATXModern Desktop1 Socket, 2-4 slots3-7 PCIe slots
Micro-ATXModern Desktop (Compact)1 Socket, 2-4 slots1-4 PCIe slots
Mini-ITXSmall Form Factor (SFF)1 Socket, 2 slots1 PCIe slot
Extended-ATX (E-ATX)Workstation/Server1-2 Sockets, 4-8+ slots4-8 PCIe slots
Standard ATX

12 x 9.6 inches

The default choice. Offers the most expansion (3-7 PCIe slots, 4 RAM slots). Ideal for gamers and future-proofing.

Micro-ATX

9.6 x 9.6 inches

The middle ground. Shorter than ATX with fewer PCIe slots (1-4). Cost-effective for budget builds.

Mini-ITX

6.7 x 6.7 inches

The specialist. Minimal expansion (1 PCIe, 2 RAM slots). Exclusively for Small Form Factor (SFF) builds.

Enthusiast & Legacy Types

Extended-ATX (E-ATX)

Larger than ATX. Used for workstations/servers with dual CPUs.

AT (Legacy)

The large predecessor to ATX (1980s-90s).

LPX (Legacy Slim)

Used a riser card for slots. Slim profile.

Mini-ATX / Pico BTX

Obsolete compact standards.

My Reflection

This portfolio demonstrated that the motherboard functions as the central nervous system of a PC. The primary takeaway was understanding form factors. Choosing between ATX, Micro-ATX, and Mini-ITX involves trade-offs between size and features; knowing the specific dimensions and limitations clarified how physical constraints influence a computer's capabilities.
